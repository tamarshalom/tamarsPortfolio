When I was thinking about what to put as my header image, I knew I did not want some random stock photo or geeky generic tech aesthetic. I wanted something that can actually say something about me, still a little geeky, but something that felt way more intentional. I am a computer science student, and a lot of what I find interesting about the field is what happens under the hood of the car. I have always thought of myself as a curious person and enjoy learning and figuring things out. I liked to understand the logic, system, and structure behind algorithms and programs that you don't always see. I wanted the image to reflect that, and give anyone who ends up on my site an immediate sense of how I think about problems. I believe my image gives off the vibes that I am a little nerdy but I also have this creative edge to me that comes out to play.

I ended up creating it in Canva, starting with a creative commons image of binary code tunnel as the base. It felt like the right visual shorthand for the coding without being too on the nose with it. From there, I layered my logo and some typography over it, keeping everything centered so that there was a clear focal point. The hardest part for me was getting the background to feel present without competing with the text. I attempted many different methods but I found that when I adjusted the opacity and contrast until it sat back enough to let the logo shine worked the best.

What I found interesting about the process especially after doing the readings was how much it reflects what Manovich talks about in Inside Photoshop. The image is not really a single thing in itself; it's a stack of independent parts that can be moved, resized, or even repurposed. That is kind of the whole point of modern digital editing and it's what ends up making it so different from something like MS paint where once everything is merged together that's it, it's committed. Davison's discussion on early bitmap editing made me appreciate how much that struggle shaped what people could even imagine making at the time. Working in layers meant I could iterate without losing track or anything in general which changed how I approached the overall design of the image. It felt a lot less like making a fixed artifact and more like assembling something flexible. More like something that could be adapted and reused across different parts of the site as it grows overall.
