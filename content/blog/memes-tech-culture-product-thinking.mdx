This post is written for **students interested in tech, computer science, and product management**. The goal is to treat memes as more than jokes: they are compressed arguments about prioritization, shipping stress, and how teams actually behave. I use three well-known templates and connect each one to problems you will keep seeing in internships, classes, and early-career roles—technical debt, sprint chaos, feature creep, launches, and the gap between “what we should do” and “what gets attention.”

Patrick Davison’s framework is useful here because it separates three layers that are easy to blur together. In his account, the **ideal** is the underlying idea or value the meme is “about”; **behavior** is what people *do* with the meme (remix, relabel, repost, crop) to keep it alive; and the **manifestation** is the concrete thing you see on the screen—the specific image, labels, and caption in front of you. The ideal shapes behavior, and behavior produces particular manifestations. Below, I walk through three memes with that lens.

## 1. Distracted Boyfriend — flashy work over fundamentals

**Know Your Meme entry (full article, history, examples):** [Distracted Boyfriend](https://knowyourmeme.com/memes/distracted-boyfriend)

**Why it lands for this audience.** In a tech or PM reading, the “boyfriend” is often the team’s attention, the “girlfriend” is unglamorous but essential work (bugs, reliability, core user needs), and the “other woman” is the shiny distraction—another feature flag, a redesign nobody asked for, or “we should add AI to everything.” The joke is **misprioritization**: everyone *says* they care about quality and users, but the eye line tells you what actually wins calendar time.

**Davison’s framework.**

- **Ideal:** The meme’s core idea is *misplaced desire or attention*—choosing what is exciting, new, or ego-boosting over what is necessary or loyal to existing commitments.
- **Behavior:** People extend the meme by **relabeling the three figures** to map onto new domains (relationships, politics, software teams). That act of substitution is the engine that keeps the format alive across communities.
- **Manifestation:** One concrete version for this audience might label the figures as **engineering team**, **fixing technical debt**, and **shipping a flashy low-priority feature**. That differs from political or dating versions because it targets how incentives and roadmaps pull teams away from fundamentals—something CS and PM students feel in group projects and internships alike.

The meme spread from a stock photo (often traced to photographer Antonio Guillem) into a global object-labeling format around 2017; Know Your Meme documents that trajectory and many example variants.

## 2. This Is Fine — calm while everything burns

  src="/blog-memes/this-is-fine.jpeg"

  width={300}
  height={168}
  caption="Reaction image from K.C. Green’s *Gunshow* comic, widely used as 'This Is Fine.'"
/>

**Know Your Meme entry:** [This Is Fine](https://knowyourmeme.com/memes/this-is-fine)

**Why it lands for this audience.** The panel captures **denial under pressure**: the build is red, the launch window is tomorrow, user complaints are spiking, and someone in the standup still says “we’re good.” For PM and engineering students, it maps cleanly onto **sprint collapse**, **last-minute scope**, or **production incidents** where the socially acceptable move is to project calm even when the system is not fine.

**Davison’s framework.**

- **Ideal:** The idea is **stoic (or performative) acceptance in the face of obvious dysfunction**—often with irony, because the audience sees the fire.
- **Behavior:** Users **repost, crop, and caption** the reaction image to match their own stressful contexts—exams, jobs, and especially “everything is on fire” work chat.
- **Manifestation:** A tech-specific version might show the dog as a PM or tech lead saying “this is fine” while Slack fills with bugs, the roadmap slips, and engineers find a serious issue before release. That is different from purely personal or political uses because it encodes the **norms of shipping culture**: protect morale, avoid panic, keep moving—even when the honest message would be “we need to stop and fix this.”

Know Your Meme traces the image to K.C. Green’s *Gunshow* (January 2013) and describes how the cropped reaction panel spread through forums and social platforms before entering general internet vocabulary.

## 3. Drakeposting (Drake / Hotline Bling) — rejecting bad choices, endorsing better ones

<Figure
  src="/blog-memes/drakeposting.jpg"
  alt="Drakeposting meme: two-panel Drake from Hotline Bling—first panel rejecting (hand out), second panel approving (pointing)."
  width={800}
  height={450}
  caption="Two-panel Drake format used to contrast 'no' vs. 'yes' options."
/>

**Know Your Meme entry:** [Drakeposting / Drake Hotline Bling](https://knowyourmeme.com/memes/drakeposting)

**Why it lands for this audience.** The format is a **binary decision meme**: top Drake refuses something; bottom Drake welcomes the alternative. For CS and product students, that maps onto everyday tradeoffs: **quick hacks vs. readable code**, **more scope vs. cutting to ship**, **AI hype-driven tickets vs. validated user problems**, or **measuring vanity metrics vs. outcomes that matter**. It is a fast way to argue for judgment without writing a essay in Slack.

**Davison’s framework.**

- **Ideal:** The meme encodes **preference and status**—one option is framed as embarrassing or shortsighted, the other as wise or socially rewarded within a community.
- **Behavior:** Participants **swap the captions** on the two panels, sometimes inverting expectations for irony. The structure stays stable while the labels change.
- **Manifestation:** A student-facing tech version might refuse **“rewrite everything in the newest framework”** but approve **“incremental refactor with tests”**—or refuse **“ship Friday at 5pm”** and approve **“scope cut + Monday deploy.”** That manifestation is distinct from music or sports jokes because it rehearses **product and engineering judgment** in a format everyone in tech already recognizes.

---

## Takeaway

These three memes work for students in tech, CS, and product not only because they are recognizable, but because their **manifestations** can be tuned to real professional tensions: misprioritization, launch stress, and binary tradeoffs. Davison’s split between ideal, behavior, and manifestation helps explain *why* the same image can travel from general humor to industry-specific critique without changing the underlying template.
